Service Value Chain Activities - Detailed Guide

1. Plan

Purpose

Planning ensures a shared understanding of the vision, current status, and improvement direction for all four dimensions and all products and services across the organization.

Key Messages

  • Shared Understanding: It is critical that all stakeholders have a common interpretation of plans, goals, and visions. This goes beyond simple agreement to genuine mutual comprehension.
  • All Four Dimensions: Planning must address organization and people, information and technology, partners and suppliers, and value streams and processes.
  • Across the Organization: Planning involves everyone, including those who may be difficult to engage.

Key Activities

  • Establishing the vision and direction for improvement
  • Understanding current status across all four dimensions
  • Communicating plans effectively to all stakeholders
  • Coordinating planning activities across products, services, and teams

2. Improve

Purpose

This activity focuses on continual improvement of products, services, and practices across all value chain activities and the four dimensions of service management.

Key Messages

  • Everything is subject to improvement—governance, value creation, practices, and all organizational aspects.
  • If things are not improving, they are getting worse; stability is not an option.
  • Continual improvement is a core component pervasive throughout the Service Value System.

Key Activities

  • Identifying improvement opportunities
  • Building business cases for improvements
  • Planning and implementing improvements
  • Measuring and evaluating improvement results
  • Coordinating improvement ideas across the organization

3. Engage

Purpose

The engage activity provides a good understanding of stakeholder needs, transparency, and continual engagement and good relationships with all stakeholders.

Key Messages

  • Understanding: Not just asking or presuming, but ensuring genuine shared comprehension of needs.
  • Transparency: Keeping things honest, clear, and easy to follow; avoiding jargon and maintaining open channels.
  • Continual Engagement: This is not a one-time effort but an ongoing process of keeping stakeholders involved.

Key Activities

  • Understanding stakeholder needs and requirements
  • Maintaining continuous communication with all stakeholders
  • Ensuring transparency in operations and decision-making
  • Managing stakeholder satisfaction and relationships
  • Capturing and reporting on service issues

4. Design and Transition

Purpose

Ensures that products and services continually meet stakeholder expectations for quality, costs, and time to market.

Key Messages

  • Both design and transition are intimately linked with stakeholder expectations.
  • Success requires knowing who the stakeholders are and what their expectations entail.
  • Delivering expectations OR changing them—modifying expectations is often the only viable way when people expect the impossible.
  • Better to change expectations at the design stage to ensure satisfaction at transition.

Key Activities

  • Designing new or changed services to meet requirements
  • Ensuring services meet quality, cost, and time-to-market targets
  • Transitioning services into live environments
  • Testing and validating service components
  • Managing stakeholder expectations throughout design and transition

5. Obtain/Build

Purpose

Ensures that service components are available when and where they are needed and meet agreed specifications.

Key Messages

  • Get the right components, at the right time, to the right place.
  • Success depends on understanding what is needed and how components will be used.
  • Reinforces the need for shared understanding across the value chain.

Key Activities

  • Acquiring or building service components
  • Ensuring components meet specifications
  • Managing suppliers and contracts
  • Quality assurance of components
  • Making components available for deployment

6. Deliver and Support

Purpose

Ensures that services are delivered and supported according to agreed specifications and stakeholders' expectations.

Key Messages

  • Deliver and support the entire service, not just software applications or individual components.
  • Services include training, documentation, intuitiveness, and ongoing updates.
  • The definition separates agreed specifications and stakeholder expectations—these rarely align perfectly.
  • Specifications may not represent shared understanding; things change; people make errors.

Key Activities

  • Delivering services to users and customers
  • Providing ongoing support and maintenance
  • Managing incidents and service requests
  • Communicating with users to keep expectations met
  • Ensuring service continuity and availability

Service Value Streams

Service value streams are specific paths through the Service Value Chain. They represent the end-to-end activities undertaken to deliver particular outcomes, such as:

  • Incident resolution
  • New feature delivery
  • Service request fulfillment
  • Change implementation

Each value stream leverages different combinations and sequences of the six SVC activities. There are multiple ways to move through the value chain, and each represents a distinct value stream tailored to specific organizational needs and contexts.

Hierarchy

  1. Service Value System (SVS) – The overarching model
  2. Service Value Chain (SVC) – The six interconnected activities
  3. Service Value Streams – Specific combinations of activities for particular outcomes
